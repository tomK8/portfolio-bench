Risk metrics

Volatility (annualised)

How wildly does the portfolio swing day to day?

Annualised volatility is the standard deviation of daily portfolio returns, scaled up to a full-year equivalent. It answers the question: if today's return is "typical", how far from average might it actually land?

σ_daily = stdev(r₁, r₂, ..., rₙ) σ_annual = σ_daily × √252 (252 ≈ trading days per year)
Worked example Suppose daily portfolio TWR returns have a standard deviation of 0.64%.
σ_annual = 0.64% × √252 = 0.64% × 15.87 = 10.2%

This means roughly two-thirds of calendar years (one standard deviation) you'd expect the portfolio to land within ±10.2% of its average annual return. About one year in six, it will swing beyond that range in either direction.
< 8%Very low — bond-heavy or highly diversified globally
8 – 12%Typical for a broadly diversified equity portfolio
12 – 20%Concentrated equity or sector-tilted portfolio
20 – 35%Single-stock or small-cap heavy
> 35%Very volatile; leveraged, crypto, or extreme concentration
Caveats Volatility is symmetric — it penalises big up days the same as big down days. A portfolio that only ever goes up, but in irregular jumps, scores "high volatility" even though it never lost money. The Sortino ratio addresses this by only penalising down days. Only weekday returns are used here; including weekend zero-fills would compress the figure artificially (√252 assumes five trading days per week).

Sharpe Ratio

How much return did you earn per unit of risk taken?

The Sharpe ratio answers: was the return worth the volatility? It compares the excess return (above the risk-free rate — i.e. what you could have earned in cash without any risk) against the total swings you had to stomach. A ratio of 1.0 means you earned one percentage point of excess return for every percentage point of annualised volatility.

Sharpe = (R_portfolio − R_risk-free) / σ_annual
Worked example Portfolio annualised return: 14.2%
Risk-free rate: 4.0% (e.g. current gilt yield)
Annualised volatility: 10.2%

Sharpe = (14.2% − 4.0%) / 10.2% = 10.2% / 10.2% = 1.0

Change the risk-free rate to 5%: Sharpe = (14.2% − 5.0%) / 10.2% = 0.90. This is why the risk-free rate input matters — always set it to your actual cash yield.
< 0Losing to cash — you took risk for a worse outcome
0 – 0.5Poor; the volatility is not compensated
0.5 – 1.0Acceptable; typical for passive equity over long periods
1.0 – 2.0Good; evidence of skill or favourable conditions
> 2.0Excellent; rare over multi-year periods
Caveats Sharpe treats all volatility as bad — it penalises big winning days just as much as big losing days. This is unrealistic; see Sortino below. It is also easily inflated over short bull-market periods (high return + low volatility = great Sharpe that won't persist). Use the trailing-1y Sharpe alongside the full-history figure to spot this.

Sortino Ratio

Like Sharpe, but only bad days count as "risk".

The Sortino ratio replaces Sharpe's denominator (total volatility) with downside deviation — the standard deviation of excess returns on days where the portfolio falls below the risk-free rate. Upside swings are not penalised. This makes Sortino more intuitive: a portfolio that makes money in large lumpy gains but has very controlled losses will score much better on Sortino than Sharpe.

σ_downside = stdev(excess returns on loss days only) Sortino = (R_portfolio − R_risk-free) / σ_downside
Worked example Same portfolio as above: annualised return 14.2%, risk-free 4.0%.
But gains come in large bursts while losses are small and steady.
Downside deviation: 6.1% (losses are tightly clustered around small negatives).

Sortino = (14.2% − 4.0%) / 6.1% = 1.67
vs Sharpe = 1.0

The gap (Sortino much higher than Sharpe) confirms the portfolio's gains are larger than its losses — exactly the profile you want.
Sortino ≈ SharpeGains and losses are roughly symmetric
Sortino >> SharpeGains dominate; positively skewed returns — good
Sortino < SharpeLosses dominate; negatively skewed — a warning sign

As absolute benchmarks, the same rough thresholds apply as Sharpe: above 1.0 is good, above 2.0 is excellent.

Max Drawdown

What is the worst loss you would have suffered buying at the peak and selling at the trough?

Max drawdown measures the largest percentage decline from any historical portfolio peak to the subsequent trough, before a new peak is reached. It captures the "left tail" — the worst-case pain a real investor would have experienced.

Max Drawdown = (Trough value − Peak value) / Peak value
Worked example Portfolio peaked at £100,000 on 3 Jan 2018.
It fell to a trough of £77,840 on 24 Dec 2018 (shown as the drawdown date).

Max drawdown = (£77,840 − £100,000) / £100,000 = −22.16%

To recover from −22.16%, the portfolio needs to gain +28.5% from the trough — not 22.16%, because the starting base is now lower.

The asymmetry of drawdown recovery gets brutal at large losses:

−10% drawdownrequires +11.1% to recover
−20% drawdownrequires +25.0% to recover
−33% drawdownrequires +50.0% to recover
−50% drawdownrequires +100.0% to recover
−75% drawdownrequires +300.0% to recover
Context matters The S&P 500 had a −55% max drawdown in 2007–2009 and −34% in the March 2020 COVID crash (which recovered in just 6 months). A portfolio with a −22% drawdown in line with broad markets during a severe bear market is very different from −22% during a calm market — it may indicate excessive concentration or poor risk management.

Recovery (days)

How long did it take to get back to where you were?

Recovery measures the number of calendar days from the trough of the maximum drawdown to the next date on which the portfolio value equalled or exceeded the pre-drawdown peak. It gives the max drawdown temporal context: a small loss that took years to recover may be more damaging to compounding than a larger loss that bounced back quickly.

Worked example Peak: £100,000 on 3 Jan 2018
Trough: £77,840 on 24 Dec 2018 (−22.16%)
Recovery back above £100,000: 11 Nov 2019

Recovery = 24 Dec 2018 → 11 Nov 2019 = 322 days

Compare: the S&P 500's COVID drawdown (Mar 2020) recovered in ~150 days. Its 2008–09 drawdown took over 1,500 days. Those two periods had similar peak losses (~34% vs ~55%) but radically different recovery profiles.

If Recovery shows —, the portfolio hasn't returned to its prior peak — you are still in the drawdown. This is useful information in itself: it means the max drawdown figure understates the ongoing loss relative to high-water mark.

Calmar Ratio

How much annual return do you earn per percentage point of worst-case loss?

The Calmar ratio divides annualised return by the absolute value of the maximum drawdown. It rewards strategies that grow steadily without catastrophic crashes, and harshly penalises boom-bust strategies that post great returns but with enormous drawdowns.

Calmar = Annualised return / |Max drawdown|
Worked example Portfolio annualised return: 9.9%
Max drawdown: −22.16%

Calmar = 9.9% / 22.16% = 0.45

A short-volatility strategy might post 20% annualised returns — until a volatility spike wipes out 80%. Calmar = 20% / 80% = 0.25, despite the impressive headline return. A boring diversified portfolio at 8% return / 15% drawdown scores 0.53 — and is arguably superior on a risk-adjusted basis.
< 0.25Poor — the drawdown risk swamps the return
0.25 – 0.5Below average
0.5 – 1.0Reasonable for a long-only equity portfolio
> 1.0Strong
> 3.0Exceptional; rare outside hedge funds

Calmar is based on a single worst event — it doesn't tell you whether that drawdown was a once-in-a-generation crisis or a recurring pattern. Always read it alongside the drawdown date and recovery time.

Valuation

EV/EBITDA

How many years of operating earnings does the market price pay for the whole business?

EV/EBITDA is the most widely used cross-company valuation multiple for non-financial businesses. It compares the total cost of acquiring the business (Enterprise Value, which includes debt) against its operating cash earnings before accounting choices and financing costs strip them away.

EV = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash EBITDA = Operating profit + Depreciation + Amortisation EV/EBITDA = EV / EBITDA (TTM)
Worked example — two companies, same P/E but different leverage Company A: market cap £2B, debt £1B, cash £0.2B, EBITDA £300M → EV = £2.8B → EV/EBITDA = 9.3×
Company B: market cap £2B, debt £0.1B, cash £0.5B, EBITDA £300M → EV = £1.6B → EV/EBITDA = 5.3×

Both have the same market cap and EBITDA, so P/E would look identical — but Company A is significantly more expensive on EV/EBITDA because a buyer inherits far more debt.
NegativeEBITDA is negative — multiple is meaningless
< 6×Cheap by historical norms (or structurally impaired)
6 – 12×Typical for a mature, stable business
12 – 20×Growth premium priced in
> 20×High-growth expectation or expensive
Not meaningful for financials Banks, insurers, and asset managers use debt as their product (deposits, insurance float). Enterprise Value conflates their investment portfolios with operational leverage, producing a nonsensical EV and therefore a nonsensical EV/EBITDA. For financials, use P/B and ROE instead.

P/B — Price to Book

What does the market pay for £1 of net assets?

Book value is shareholders' equity on the balance sheet: everything the company owns (assets) minus everything it owes (liabilities). P/B compares the market's valuation to this accounting value of the business.

Book value per share = Shareholders' equity / Shares outstanding P/B = Share price / Book value per share
Worked example Share price: £4.20
Shareholders' equity: £2.1B, shares outstanding: 1B
Book value per share: £2.10

P/B = 4.20 / 2.10 = 2.0×

The market is willing to pay £2 for every £1 of net assets, implying it believes the business earns returns above its cost of equity and has value beyond its balance sheet.
< 1.0×Market values firm below net assets — value trap or genuine bargain; investigate why
1 – 2×Typical for asset-heavy or capital-intensive businesses
2 – 5×Typical for profitable companies with brand or IP value
> 10×Asset-light businesses (tech, platforms) where intangibles dominate
Caveat P/B is most meaningful for banks (where book value ≈ regulatory capital and is audited conservatively) and least meaningful for tech companies where the most valuable assets — software, patents, brand, talent — don't appear on the balance sheet at all.

P/S — Price to Sales (TTM)

What does the market pay for £1 of revenue?

P/S is the valuation multiple of last resort: it works even when a company has no earnings or book value. It's especially used for high-growth companies where profitability is deferred and investors are pricing future margin expansion.

P/S = Market Cap / Revenue (trailing 12 months)
Worked example SaaS company: market cap £800M, trailing revenue £200M.
P/S = 800 / 200 = 4.0×

If the company is growing at 35%/year and you expect 25% profit margins at maturity, at 4× P/S you're paying a modest premium. If it's growing at 5% with perpetually thin margins, 4× is expensive.
< 1×Very cheap relative to revenue — often mature or distressed
1 – 3×Reasonable for profitable, moderate-growth businesses
3 – 8×Typical for high-growth tech/SaaS
> 10×Priced for very rapid growth; only justified with margin expansion
Caveat P/S ignores profitability entirely. A company with revenue of £1B but structurally negative margins (e.g. subsidising customers to win market share) can still be a terrible investment at any P/S. Always read P/S alongside FCF margin trajectory.

Enterprise Value

What would it actually cost to buy the whole business?

Enterprise Value is the theoretical acquisition price of a company. When you buy the equity (market cap), you implicitly inherit the company's debt — but you also receive any cash it holds. EV accounts for both.

EV = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash and equivalents
Worked example — debt changes the real price Market cap: £5B. Total debt: £1.5B. Cash: £0.8B.
EV = £5B + £1.5B − £0.8B = £5.7B

You pay £5B for the shares, but you'll also need to service (or repay) £1.5B of debt, offset by the £0.8B of cash you'd collect. The true cost is £5.7B.

EV is mainly useful as the numerator in EV/EBITDA and EV/Sales — ratios that let you compare companies with very different debt levels, because both numerator and denominator are independent of capital structure choices. A cash-rich, debt-free company has EV below market cap; a highly leveraged company has EV far above it.

Cash flow

Operating Cash Flow (TTM)

Cash the business actually generates from its core operations.

Operating cash flow (OCF) is the cash a business produces from its day-to-day operations — before investing that cash in new assets or returning it to shareholders. It is widely considered a more reliable indicator of earning power than net income, because it strips out non-cash accounting items like depreciation and stock-based compensation.

OCF can be positive even when net income is negative (a company burning cash on depreciation of old assets) and vice versa (a company booking profits but with poor cash collection). The gap between the two is worth investigating.

Not meaningful for financials For banks, insurance companies and asset managers, "operating cash flow" encompasses their entire investment activity — it doesn't have the same meaning as for an industrial or technology business.

Free Cash Flow & FCF Margin

Cash left over after maintaining and growing the business — available to shareholders.

Free Cash Flow is the cash remaining after spending on capital expenditures (Capex) — the factories, equipment, servers, and infrastructure needed to run and grow the business. FCF is the money a company can return to shareholders via dividends or buybacks, use to repay debt, or invest in acquisitions.

FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditure FCF Margin = FCF / Revenue
Worked example — capital intensity matters Company A (software): OCF £200M, Capex £15M → FCF = £185M (FCF margin: 37%)
Company B (telecoms): OCF £200M, Capex £160M → FCF = £40M (FCF margin: 8%)

Same operating cash flow, but Company A converts 37p of every £1 of revenue into free cash while Company B converts just 8p. Company A is a dramatically better business at the same revenue level.
FCF Margin < 5%Capital-intensive; thin; limited shareholder returns
5 – 15%Typical for industrials, retailers, manufacturers
15 – 25%Good; healthy platform or technology business
> 25%Exceptional; software, platforms, asset-light businesses

EBITDA

Operating profit before the accountants and the CFO's capital choices get involved.

EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation and Amortisation. It attempts to measure the cash-generating ability of the core business by stripping out items that can vary widely between companies without reflecting operational performance:

  • Interest: depends on how much debt the company chose to take on
  • Taxes: vary by jurisdiction and tax planning
  • Depreciation & Amortisation: non-cash accounting charges for capital already spent

EBITDA is a useful bridge metric — it's why EV/EBITDA lets you compare a US company with a German one despite different tax rates, or a capital-intensive manufacturer with an asset-light tech firm on the same scale.

The Buffett critique Warren Buffett famously dislikes EBITDA, noting that depreciation is real: it represents assets wearing out that will need to be replaced. A business that spends £500M on equipment every year can't pretend that cost doesn't exist just because the accountants spread it over a decade. FCF is a more honest measure for capital-intensive businesses. For asset-light businesses (software, IP), EBITDA ≈ FCF and the critique is less relevant.
Quality & returns

ROIC — Return on Invested Capital

The single most important measure of whether a business is creating or destroying value.

ROIC measures how efficiently a company converts invested capital (equity plus debt, minus surplus cash) into after-tax operating profit. It is the closest single metric to the answer of "is this a good business?" — independent of how it's financed.

NOPAT = Operating profit × (1 − effective tax rate) Invested Capital = Equity + Debt − Surplus cash ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital
Worked example — ROIC vs WACC is what matters Company: NOPAT £120M, invested capital £800M → ROIC = 15%
Cost of capital (WACC): 9%

ROIC (15%) > WACC (9%) → every £1 reinvested creates £0.15 of after-tax profit on capital that costs £0.09. The spread of 6% per year compounds into substantial value creation.

If ROIC = 7% and WACC = 9%, reinvestment actively destroys value — the company would be better off returning cash to shareholders.
< cost of capitalValue-destroying; every pound reinvested makes shareholders poorer
10 – 15%Average; roughly in line with cost of capital
15 – 25%Good; sustainable competitive advantage likely
> 25%Exceptional moat; rare and often priced in
Not meaningful for financials Banks and insurers hold debt as their product. Their "invested capital" is regulatory capital, not operational investment, so ROIC is not comparable to non-financial companies. Use ROE for financials instead.

Return on Equity (ROE)

How much profit does the business generate for each pound of shareholders' equity?

ROE = Net Income / Shareholders' Equity
Worked example Net income: £150M. Shareholders' equity: £900M.
ROE = 150 / 900 = 16.7%

The company generates £16.70 of profit for every £100 shareholders have invested in the business — a return most investors would consider good.

ROE is the primary profitability measure for banks and financial companies, where regulatory equity is the key input. For non-financials, prefer ROIC, because ROE can be mechanically inflated by taking on more debt (borrowed money counts as neither equity nor capital).

The leverage trap A company with £200M equity and £800M debt will have a much higher ROE than a competitor with the same earnings but £600M equity and £400M debt — purely because the first company used more leverage. High ROE driven by debt isn't the same as high ROE driven by operational excellence. Always check Debt/Equity alongside ROE.

Return on Assets (ROA)

How efficiently does the business use its total asset base?

ROA = Net Income / Total Assets

ROA measures profitability relative to everything the company owns — property, equipment, cash, receivables — regardless of how those assets are funded. It is not distorted by leverage in the way ROE is. A business with the same earnings but twice as many assets is less efficient.

< 2%Capital-intensive industries (mining, telecoms, utilities)
2 – 5%Typical for manufacturers and retailers
5 – 10%Good; asset-efficient operation
> 10%Exceptional; asset-light businesses (software, platforms)

Margins — Gross, Operating, and Net (Profit)

How much of each pound of revenue survives each layer of costs?

The three margin metrics form a "waterfall" from revenue down to profit, each stripping out a different layer of cost:

Gross Margin = (Revenue − COGS) / Revenue Operating Margin = Operating Income / Revenue (after overhead, before interest & tax) Net Margin = Net Income / Revenue (after everything)
Worked example — following the waterfall Revenue: £1,000
Cost of Goods Sold: £400 → Gross margin: 60%
Overheads (salaries, R&D, marketing): £300 → Operating profit: £300 → Operating margin: 30%
Interest + taxes: £120 → Net income: £180 → Net (profit) margin: 18%

Each layer tests a different thing. The gap between gross and operating margin reveals how much overhead the business carries. The gap between operating and net margin reveals the cost of its debt (interest) and its effective tax rate.
Net (profit) margin benchmarks by sector
Software / SaaS15 – 40%+
Pharmaceuticals10 – 25%
Consumer brands8 – 18%
Retail2 – 6%
Grocers / supermarkets1 – 4%

Gross margin is most useful for assessing pricing power and supply-chain efficiency. A falling gross margin means either input costs are rising faster than prices or competitors are forcing price cuts. It is harder to fake than net margin.

Leverage

Net Debt / EBITDA

How many years of operating earnings would it take to repay all debt?

Net Debt/EBITDA is the primary leverage metric for non-financial businesses. It answers the simple question: if the company stopped investing and returned all operating cash to paying down debt, how long would it take?

Net Debt / EBITDA = (Total Debt − Cash) / EBITDA
Worked example Total debt: £1.2B. Cash: £0.3B. Net Debt: £0.9B.
EBITDA (TTM): £300M.
Net Debt/EBITDA = 0.9B / 0.3B = 3.0× — three years' earnings to clear the debt.

Negative value (e.g. −1.5×): the company holds more cash than debt — it's a net creditor, not a net debtor. This is a strong balance sheet position.
NegativeNet cash — no meaningful debt burden
0 – 1×Very conservative; easily managed
1 – 2×Comfortable for most businesses
2 – 3×Moderate leverage; acceptable in stable sectors
3 – 4×Elevated; limits flexibility, especially in downturns
> 4×High leverage; dependent on stable cash flow; risk of distress

Net Debt

How much debt does the company carry after netting out its cash?

Net Debt = Total Debt (short-term + long-term) − Cash and equivalents

Net Debt is the starting point for assessing balance sheet risk. A company with £2B of debt but £1.8B of cash has a net debt of only £200M and is in a very different position to a company with £2B of debt and £50M of cash — even though their gross debt is the same.

A negative Net Debt means the company is a net creditor — it holds more cash and liquid assets than it owes. This is common among capital-light tech businesses that have not chosen to return the excess to shareholders.

Debt / Equity

What proportion of the business is funded by debt vs shareholders' money?

Debt / Equity = Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity
Worked example Total debt: £600M. Shareholders' equity: £400M.
D/E = 600 / 400 = 1.5× (or 150%)

For every £1 of shareholders' money, the company has borrowed £1.50 — it is more debt-funded than equity-funded.

Higher D/E amplifies both gains and losses. In good times, leverage magnifies returns on equity. In downturns, it can accelerate distress because interest must still be paid regardless of revenue. Sectors with stable, predictable cash flows (utilities, supermarkets) tolerate high D/E; cyclical businesses (mining, construction) with volatile earnings are more exposed.

Dividends

Dividend Yield

The income return from holding the stock, ignoring price changes.

Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend per Share / Current Share Price
Worked example Share price: £2.50. Annual dividend: £0.19 per share.
Yield = 0.19 / 2.50 = 7.6%

This is the running income return — comparable to a bond coupon. If the share price falls to £2.00, the yield rises to 9.5% (the dividend hasn't changed, but you're buying more income for less money — or the market is worried the dividend will be cut).
High yield ≠ safe income A very high yield (say >8%) often signals that the market expects a dividend cut. The yield is calculated from the last declared dividend; if the company is struggling, the next dividend may be smaller or suspended entirely. Always check the payout ratio and free cash flow coverage before relying on a high yield.

Payout Ratio

What fraction of earnings is being paid out as dividends?

Payout Ratio = Annual Dividends / Net Income
Worked example Annual dividends: £200M. Net income (TTM): £760M.
Payout ratio = 200 / 760 = 26% — very sustainable, leaves room to grow the dividend.

If the same company earned only £150M:
Payout ratio = 200 / 150 = 133% — paying out more than it earns. This is only sustainable short-term by drawing on reserves or borrowing.
< 30%Low; lots of retained earnings for reinvestment or future dividend growth
30 – 60%Balanced; typical for mature dividend payers
60 – 80%Elevated; still maintainable if earnings are stable
80 – 100%High; little buffer against an earnings dip
> 100%Paying from reserves or debt; likely unsustainable
Technicals & sentiment

Moving Averages — 50d MA and 200d MA

Where is the trend, and is the stock above or below it?

A moving average smooths out daily price noise by averaging the closing price over a rolling window. The 50-day MA captures the short-term trend; the 200-day MA captures the long-term trend.

Common signals Price above 200d MA: broadly bullish — the long-term trend is intact.
Price below 200d MA: bearish — the long-term trend has broken down.
Golden Cross: 50d MA crosses above 200d MA — often cited as a bullish regime signal.
Death Cross: 50d MA crosses below 200d MA — bearish regime signal.
Caution Moving averages are lagging indicators — they describe where the price has been, not where it's going. They are most useful as context (e.g. "this dip is within a healthy uptrend") rather than standalone trading signals. Over long holding periods (years) they have limited predictive power for fundamental investors.

Short % of Float

How many traders are betting the price will fall?

Short selling involves borrowing shares and selling them, hoping to buy them back cheaper later. The short float is the percentage of freely tradeable shares (the "float") that are currently sold short. It is a measure of aggregate bearish sentiment.

< 2%Normal; low short interest, no significant bearish consensus
2 – 5%Moderate; some sceptics but no unusual concern
5 – 10%Elevated; meaningful bearish conviction in the market
> 10%High; the market is actively betting against this stock
> 20%Extreme; classic short-squeeze setup OR genuine distress

The short squeeze: if a heavily shorted stock rises sharply, short sellers must buy shares to close their positions, which pushes the price higher still — a feedback loop. GameStop (January 2021) is the most famous recent example. High short interest is therefore not unambiguously bearish: it also represents potential buying pressure if the thesis fails.

Institutional Ownership (%)

What proportion of shares is held by large professional investors?

Institutional investors include pension funds, mutual funds, hedge funds, insurance companies, and sovereign wealth funds. Their ownership percentage indicates how much of the stock is held by large, well-resourced investors who have done professional due diligence.

< 20%Low; mainly retail-held; may be under-researched or too small for institutions
20 – 50%Moderate; on the institutional radar
50 – 80%High; widely held by professional investors
> 80%Very high; dominated by institutions — price can move sharply if they rotate

High institutional ownership is often read as a quality signal — large funds have research teams and are unlikely to hold poor-quality businesses. But it also creates a concentration risk: if several large holders decide to sell simultaneously (e.g. due to an index rebalancing or a negative earnings surprise), the selling pressure can be severe and the exit narrow.
